Foundation

Brand & Logo Principles

What makes a dining app brand timeless, adaptable, and culturally durable
◯
The Icon Must Work at 16px
Your app icon will appear as a 60x60 dot on a home screen surrounded by 35 other apps. If the mark is not instantly legible at that size, it does not exist. Test every concept at 16px, 32px, and 64px before committing.
Rule: One shape. One idea. No more than two elements.
⬛
Black & White First
A great logo works in pure black on white and pure white on black before it earns color. If it needs color to make sense, the mark itself is not strong enough. Build in monochrome, add color as a reward.
Rule: Design in black. Add color last.
⟲
Timeless Over Trendy
Gradients, 3D effects, and drop shadows date a brand within 18 months. Flat geometric marks, clean wordmarks, and single-weight iconography have survived decades. The Helvetica wordmark. The Nike swoosh. The CBS eye. All flat. All still here.
Rule: If a design trend has a name, avoid it.
⊕
The System Not the Logo
A brand is not a logo. It is a system — mark, wordmark, color, typography, voice, motion, and space working together. Pick a typeface that will live in every email, notification, and screen. It will be seen more than the logo itself.
Rule: Choose one display font and one body font. Use them everywhere.
⬡
Adaptable Across Contexts
Your mark will appear on a dark app header, a white email, a TikTok thumbnail, a tote bag, a wax seal on an invitation card, and an OG social share card. Design a mark that welcomes all of those surfaces without modification.
Rule: Build a horizontal lockup, a stacked lockup, and an icon-only mark from day one.
◈
Avoid Food Clichés
Forks, knives, plates, chef hats, and speech bubbles are the visual landfill of the food app space. Every food app since 2010 has used them. The brands that stand out — Eater, Bon Appétit, Momofuku — use abstract geometry, editorial photography, and typography as the identity, not kitchen objects.
Rule: If it would be on a restaurant menu, it should not be your logo.
▲
Typography IS the Brand
For a named brand like yours — one where the name is the product — the wordmark is the logo. A beautifully set name in the right typeface at the right weight is more powerful than any icon. Think Slack, Stripe, Notion. The name IS the mark.
Rule: Spend 80% of identity budget on getting the wordmark perfect.
◐
One Accent Color Rule
The most durable food and lifestyle brands use one dominant color with one accent. Eater uses red. Bon Appétit uses cream and black. The New York Times Cooking uses a single saturated red. One color creates recognition. Two creates confusion. Five creates noise.
Rule: Pick one color that owns an emotion. Use it once, powerfully.

Strategic Recommendation

The final decision framework

Three questions to narrow from 8 to 1
If SEO and brand clarity win →
Dinemate or Tablefolk. Both are invented compounds with zero SERP competition, strong verbal identity, and timeless logo potential. Either will rank #1 for their own name from day one.
If the verb and the product action win →
Dinevite. The name IS the primary user action. "I'll dinevite you" is a sentence that writes the product brief, the onboarding, and the push notification in three words.
If personality and virality win →
Disco Dine or Fork Friends. Highest social shareability of any finalist. The brand writes itself. The risk is trend-dating — offset by building the visual identity around timeless geometry, not disco references.
One final thought
The strongest brand in this list is the one you can say out loud, use as a verb, wear on a t-shirt, and put on a wax seal simultaneously. By that test — Dinemate and Tablefolk are the most durable. Dinevite is the most functional. Disco Dine is the most memorable. None of these is wrong. The right choice is the one that sounds like you.
